Experiment Details

PCR Amplification of Target Gene

Date: March 6, 2025
Time: 10:30 AM

Aim

To amplify the target gene sequence from the provided DNA sample using Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) for subsequent cloning and expression studies.

Materials

  • Thermal cycler (Bio-Rad T100)
  • PCR tubes (0.2 mL)
  • Micropipettes and sterile tips
  • DNA template (100 ng/μL)
  • Forward primer (10 μM): 5'-ATGCTAGCTAGCTAGCTAG-3'
  • Reverse primer (10 μM): 5'-GCATGCATGCATGCATGCAT-3'
  • dNTP mix (10 mM each)
  • Taq DNA polymerase (5 U/μL)
  • 10X PCR buffer
  • MgCl₂ (25 mM)
  • Nuclease-free water
  • Ice bucket

Protocols

1. PCR Reaction Setup

Materials: PCR tubes, micropipettes, DNA template, primers, dNTPs, Taq polymerase, PCR buffer, MgCl₂, nuclease-free water
Procedure:
  • 1
    Thaw all reagents on ice and mix gently before use.
  • 2
    Label PCR tubes appropriately for each reaction.
  • 3
    Prepare the PCR master mix in a sterile microcentrifuge tube by adding the following components in order: 36.5 μL nuclease-free water, 5 μL 10X PCR buffer, 3 μL MgCl₂, 1 μL dNTP mix, 1 μL forward primer, 1 μL reverse primer, 0.5 μL Taq polymerase.
  • 4
    Mix the master mix gently by pipetting up and down.
  • 5
    Aliquot 48 μL of master mix into each labeled PCR tube.
  • 6
    Add 2 μL of DNA template to each tube and mix gently.
Observations/Notes: The master mix appeared clear and colorless. All components were added in the correct order to prevent premature enzyme activity.

2. PCR Cycling Conditions

Materials: Thermal cycler, prepared PCR tubes
Procedure:
  • 1
    Place PCR tubes in the thermal cycler.
  • 2
    Program the thermal cycler with the following conditions:
    • Initial denaturation: 95°C for 3 minutes
    • 30 cycles of:
      • Denaturation: 95°C for 30 seconds
      • Annealing: 58°C for 30 seconds
      • Extension: 72°C for 1 minute
    • Final extension: 72°C for 5 minutes
    • Hold: 4°C indefinitely
  • 3
    Start the PCR program and allow it to complete.
Observations/Notes: The thermal cycler was functioning properly. The program ran for approximately 2 hours and 15 minutes.

3. Gel Electrophoresis Analysis

Materials: Agarose, TAE buffer, ethidium bromide, gel casting tray, electrophoresis chamber, power supply, loading dye, DNA ladder, PCR products
Procedure:
  • 1
    Prepare a 1% agarose gel in 1X TAE buffer.
  • 2
    Add ethidium bromide to a final concentration of 0.5 μg/mL.
  • 3
    Cast the gel and allow it to solidify.
  • 4
    Mix 5 μL of each PCR product with 1 μL of 6X loading dye.
  • 5
    Load samples and DNA ladder into the gel.
  • 6
    Run the gel at 100V for 45 minutes.
  • 7
    Visualize the gel under UV light and document the results.
Observations/Notes: The gel ran smoothly without any issues. The bands were clearly visible under UV light.

Results

The PCR amplification was successful, yielding a single band of the expected size (approximately 850 bp) for all samples. The negative control showed no amplification, confirming the absence of contamination. The intensity of the bands varied slightly between samples, with sample 2 showing the strongest amplification.

Gel image is attached in the lab notebook. The quantification of the PCR products using a spectrophotometer showed concentrations ranging from 25-40 ng/μL.

Discussion

The successful amplification of the target gene indicates that the PCR conditions were optimized correctly. The annealing temperature of 58°C provided sufficient specificity, as evidenced by the single band observed for each sample. The absence of non-specific bands suggests that the primer design was effective.

The variation in band intensity between samples may be attributed to differences in the initial DNA template quality or concentration. Sample 2, which showed the strongest amplification, was extracted more recently than the other samples, suggesting that DNA degradation might affect PCR efficiency.

The PCR products obtained are suitable for downstream applications such as cloning and sequencing. However, for applications requiring higher purity, an additional purification step might be necessary to remove residual primers and dNTPs.

Conclusion

The PCR protocol successfully amplified the target gene from all DNA samples. The optimized conditions provided good specificity and yield, making the PCR products suitable for subsequent cloning experiments. For future experiments, it would be beneficial to standardize the DNA extraction method to ensure more consistent template quality across samples.

Next steps include restriction digestion of the PCR products and ligation into the expression vector, followed by transformation into competent E. coli cells.
